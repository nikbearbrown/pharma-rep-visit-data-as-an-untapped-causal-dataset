## Glossary

A working reference for the principal terms this book uses. One line each; see the named chapter for full development.

**do-operator** — `do(X = x)` denotes setting X by external intervention, severing all incoming arrows to X; it makes `P(Y | do(X))` differ from the observed `P(Y | X)`. *(Chapter 3.)*

**Pearl's Ladder** — The three-rung taxonomy of causal questions: association (seeing), intervention (doing), counterfactual (imagining); each strictly more powerful, and Rung-1 data alone cannot answer a Rung-2 question. *(Chapter 3.)*

**Instrument / IV** — A variable (here, training-cohort timing) that affects treatment but reaches the outcome only through it, used to identify a causal effect from confounded data. *(Chapter 4.)*

**Exclusion restriction** — The IV assumption that the instrument affects the outcome *only* through the treatment — e.g., cohort timing changes prescribing only via the message, not via selling skill. *(Chapter 4.)*

**Difference-in-differences (DiD)** — Estimating a treatment effect by comparing the change over time in a treated group against the change in an untreated comparison group. *(Chapter 10.)*

**TWFE** — Two-way fixed effects; the classic DiD regression that is biased under heterogeneous, staggered treatment timing, motivating the modern estimators. *(Chapter 10.)*

**Collider** — A node with two arrows pointing into it; conditioning on it opens a non-causal path and induces bias (e.g., dwell time as a post-treatment collider). *(Chapter 6.)*

**Berkson selection** — Selection-induced bias from conditioning on a collider on the sampling frame — here, analyzing only the physicians who consented to be logged. *(Chapters 6 and 13.)*

**Markov equivalence / CPDAG** — A set of graphs that imply the same conditional independencies and are indistinguishable from observational data; a CPDAG represents the class, leaving some edges undirected. *(Chapter 7.)*

**Mediation (NDE / NIE / CDE)** — Decomposing a total effect into the natural direct, natural indirect, and controlled direct effects through specified pathways. *(Chapters 5 and 11.)*

**SCM** — Structural causal model; a system of equations plus a graph that supports interventional and counterfactual queries. *(Chapters 5 and 9.)*

**Counterfactual** — A Rung-3 query about a specific unit in a world contrary to fact (for *this* physician, which message next week), computed via abduction–action–prediction on an SCM. *(Chapters 3 and 9.)*

**Causal forest** — A random-forest method (generalized random forests) that estimates heterogeneous treatment effects — the CATE surface — across units. *(Chapter 11.)*

**Double ML** — Double/debiased machine learning; uses ML nuisance models with orthogonalization and cross-fitting to estimate a causal parameter, e.g., slide-ordering effects. *(Chapter 11.)*

**Qini / persuadables** — Uplift-modeling tools; persuadables are units whose outcome the treatment actually changes, and the Qini curve measures how well a ranking concentrates them. *(Chapter 12.)*

**KEBN** — Knowledge Engineering with Bayesian Networks; the discipline of building causal structure and parameters by structured elicitation from domain experts. *(Chapters 8 and 9.)*

**FCI** — Fast Causal Inference; a discovery algorithm that does not assume causal sufficiency, returns a PAG, and honestly leaves edges unoriented under latent confounding or selection. *(Chapter 13.)*

**NRx** — New prescriptions; the prescribing outcome, sourced at the NPI level from data such as IQVIA Xponent or Symphony. *(Chapters 1–2.)*

**Veeva CLM** — Closed-loop marketing; the interactive approved-slide presentation served by the CRM whose media player writes the passive telemetry. *(Chapter 2.)*

**Consent opt-out** — A physician's election (governed by fields such as `CLM_EXPLICIT_OPT_IN_vod`) that suppresses or anonymizes logging, creating the selection collider of Chapter 13. *(Chapter 13.)*
